# C1 Human-Readable Candidate

Claim: Liquidation reveals the trillion was never there

Status: human-readable rewrite after low-level reader checks.

## Thread

1. A billionaire's "trillion" is not cash. It is mostly stock. And if you try to sell all that stock, the selling itself can push the price down.

2. Net worth is usually calculated like this: current share price × number of shares owned.

3. But the current share price comes from small trades. It tells you what a few shares sold for, not what a giant block of shares would sell for.

4. If everyone knows a huge block must be sold, buyers wait or demand a discount. Each sale can lower the next sale price. The act of turning the stock into cash changes the value you are trying to measure.

5. So the claim is not "there is no wealth." There is real wealth. The claim is: the headline number was never a pile of cash ready to redistribute.

6. The strongest objection is true: selling would still raise a lot of money. Probably a huge amount. Just not necessarily anything close to the headline net worth.

7. The mistake is treating "what the market says the shares are worth right now" as "cash you can collect by selling all of them."

8. Falsifier: if huge forced stock sales usually converted into cash near the starting market value without lowering the price, this argument would fail.

## Human-readable intent

The reader should come away with this plain-English version:

"A billionaire's net worth is mostly a market estimate, not cash. If you try to sell all the assets at once, the sale itself changes the price. So liquidation would yield real money, but much less than the headline number."
